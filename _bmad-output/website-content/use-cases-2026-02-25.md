## Carbon Tax Impact Assessment

### The scenario

A ministry evaluation team needs to assess the distributional impact of a proposed carbon tax reform with multiple redistribution options. They need results broken down by income decile, household type, and region — ready for a director's briefing.

### Without ReformLab

1. Download latest INSEE household survey data (too large for Excel)
2. Write cleaning scripts to extract relevant variables
3. Run OpenFisca for baseline tax-benefit calculations
4. Write separate scripts for energy consumption modeling by income group
5. Manually join results across tools
6. Build Excel charts for the briefing
7. Document methodology in a separate Word file
8. Repeat for each redistribution variant

**Result:** Weeks of work. Results that are hard to reproduce. Methodology documentation that's always incomplete.

### With ReformLab

1. Open the carbon tax template
2. Set parameters: tax rate, energy categories, exemptions
3. Define three redistribution variants as scenario diffs
4. Click Run
5. Compare all variants side by side — distributional charts, winners/losers tables, fiscal cost

**Result:** Same-day analysis. Full methodology manifest generated automatically. Easy to re-run when parameters change.

## Energy Renovation Subsidy Evaluation

### The scenario

An evaluation department needs to assess who benefits from an energy renovation subsidy program. The program targets low-income homeowners with poorly insulated housing. The question: does the money actually reach the intended population?

### The challenge

This requires combining income data, housing stock data, energy performance certificates, and subsidy eligibility rules — datasets that live in different systems with different identifiers and formats.

### With ReformLab

- Connect income and housing data through the open data pipeline
- Define subsidy eligibility rules using the template system
- Run distributional analysis: which income deciles actually qualify, which housing types are covered, what's the geographic distribution
- Compare the "intended" targeting (who the policy was designed for) against the "actual" targeting (who qualifies based on real data distributions)
- Adjust eligibility thresholds and see how coverage changes

## Social Electric Vehicle Leasing

### The scenario

France launched a social leasing program for electric vehicles — subsidized leases for lower-income households. The question: who qualifies? Which income brackets benefit most? Is the targeting efficient?

### The challenge

Answering this means combining household income data, vehicle ownership patterns, commuting distances, and program eligibility rules. The data exists in public sources but connecting it requires significant technical effort.

### With ReformLab

- Load synthetic French household population with income and vehicle ownership attributes
- Define leasing program eligibility (income ceiling, household composition rules)
- Run simulation: who qualifies, what's the fiscal cost, what's the distributional profile of beneficiaries
- Test alternative targeting: what if the income ceiling changes? What if rural households get priority?
- Generate comparison report for three targeting variants

## Multi-Year Carbon Tax Phase-In

### The scenario

A researcher is modeling a carbon tax that increases gradually over five years (from 44 to 100 EUR/tCO2). They need to show how distributional effects evolve as the tax rises and as households adapt (vehicle replacement, heating system changes).

### Why single-year tools aren't enough

A one-shot simulation shows the impact at a single tax level. But the real question is: what happens over time as the fleet turns over, buildings get insulated, and the tax rate climbs? You need year-to-year state tracking.

### With ReformLab

- Define the tax phase-in schedule in YAML (year 1: 44, year 2: 55, ... year 5: 100)
- The dynamic orchestrator runs year by year, carrying forward household state
- Vintage tracking shows: which heating systems were replaced, which vehicles aged out, how the fleet composition shifts
- Cumulative distributional charts show the full five-year trajectory — not just a snapshot
- Run manifests document every year's computation for reproducibility

## Academic Publication Workflow

### The scenario

Marco, a university economist, needs distributional impact results for a journal submission on carbon tax incidence in France. His co-author is at a different institution. They need identical results from their respective machines.

### With ReformLab

1. Marco configures the simulation in a Jupyter notebook using the Python API
2. Runs the analysis; results generate automatically with a run manifest
3. Sends the YAML config, notebook, and manifest to his co-author
4. Co-author runs `pip install reformlab`, opens the notebook, executes it
5. Gets identical results — verified by comparing manifest hashes

**What makes this work:** Deterministic execution (same inputs = same outputs), pinned dependency versions in the manifest, synthetic population built from public data (no restricted data dependency).

## Election Proposal Comparison (Vision)

### The scenario

Before a national election, citizens want to understand how different candidates' environmental policy proposals would affect their household and people like them.

### The vision (post-MVP)

A public web application built on ReformLab lets citizens:

1. Answer a short questionnaire: income, region, housing type, commuting pattern, household composition
2. See personalized results: "Under Candidate A's carbon tax, your household pays X more but receives Y in rebates"
3. See the bigger picture: distributional charts showing effects by income group
4. Compare candidates side by side
5. Share results on social media

**Why this matters:** Policy proposals are opaque. Most people can't assess how a carbon tax or subsidy reform would affect them. This makes impact concrete and personal.

**Status:** This is a Phase 3 vision feature. The underlying simulation capabilities are being built now in MVP.
